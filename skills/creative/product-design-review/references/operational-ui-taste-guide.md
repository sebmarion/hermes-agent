# Operational UI Taste Guide

Operational UI is judged by speed, confidence, and low cognitive load.

## Good taste for operations

- Quiet base surfaces with strong content hierarchy.
- Dense but readable rows/lists where throughput matters.
- Numeric alignment for quantities, totals, prices, and statuses.
- One dominant action at a decision point.
- Status colors reserved for status.
- Clear grouping without turning every group into a giant card.
- Familiar controls over novelty.
- Copy that tells the next action, not implementation state.

## Bad taste for operations

- Decorative patterns under dense text.
- Multiple accent gradients in one viewport.
- Provider/category colors that look like warnings.
- Oversized cards that hide half the active work.
- Low-contrast metadata and labels.
- Action bars that look like another data card.
- Loading spinners in the middle of static rows without explanation.
- Every chip/badge having the same visual weight.

## Restaurant / POS / procurement specifics

Design for someone standing, moving, interrupted, and using a small/touch screen.

Prioritize:
1. Current task / next action.
2. Blockers and exceptions.
3. Supplier/order grouping.
4. Quantities and pack math.
5. Totals and deltas.
6. Secondary details.

Use:
- compact group headers;
- quiet dividers;
- clear plus/minus touch zones;
- sticky review/send action when useful;
- explicit error and warning banners only when needed.

Avoid:
- decorative glow or glass effects;
- unnecessary visual separation for every line;
- hiding totals/actions below tall cards;
- status colors as normal decoration.

## A shippable operational screen should feel

- calm;
- fast;
- trustworthy;
- readable under pressure;
- visually coherent with the rest of the product;
- boring in the right places and emphatic only where decisions happen.
